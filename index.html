📜What is a Bond?

A bond is an IOU. You lend money to a government or company, and they promise to pay you back with interest. It's like being the bank — instead of borrowing money, you're the one lending it.

💡 The Friend Analogy
Your friend asks to borrow $1,000 for 10 years. They promise to pay you $50 every year as a thank-you (the coupon), and give you the full $1,000 back at the end (the face value). That's a bond at 5%.
📖Key Terms
🏷️
Face Value (Par)
The amount you get back at maturity. Usually $1,000. Think of it as the sticker price.
💵
Coupon Rate
The annual interest rate the bond pays, based on face value. A 5% coupon on a $1,000 bond = $50/year.
📅
Maturity
When you get your money back. Ranges from 3 months (T-bills) to 30 years (long bonds).
📈
Yield
Your actual return based on what you paid. If you bought a 5% coupon bond at a discount ($950), your yield is higher than 5%.
⚖️
Duration
How sensitive your bond is to rate changes. Longer duration = bigger price swings. It's like a longer lever on the seesaw.
🎲
Credit Risk
The chance the borrower can't pay you back. US Treasuries = almost zero risk. Corporate junk bonds = higher risk, higher yield.

⚠️The 3 Risks Every Bond Investor Faces
1. Interest Rate Risk
When rates rise, your bond's price falls. The longer the maturity, the harder the hit. A 30-year bond might drop 20% if rates jump 1%, while a 2-year bond barely moves.
2. Inflation Risk
If your bond pays 4% but inflation is 5%, you're losing 1% of purchasing power every year. Your check comes in the mail, but it buys less groceries each time.
3. Credit / Default Risk
The borrower might not pay you back. US Treasuries? Almost zero risk. A startup's bond? They might go bankrupt. The yield premium on riskier bonds compensates for this — but sometimes it's not enough.
